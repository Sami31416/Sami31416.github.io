1. A refrigerator has a coefficient of performance K = 4.6. It removes 250 J of heat from the cold reservoir in each cycle. What amount of work must be supplied to run the refrigerator in each cycle?
A
46 J
B
54 J
C
56 J
D
75 J
Answer Explanation (Click to expand)
▼

Correct Answer

B) 54 J

Formula

Coefficient of Performance (K) = Qc / W
Where Qc = heat removed from cold reservoir, W = work done

Process

  1. Given: K = 4.6, Qc = 250 J
  2. Using formula: W = Qc / K
  3. W = 250 J / 4.6 ≈ 54.35 J
  4. Rounded to nearest whole number: 54 J

Reasoning

The coefficient of performance (K) for a refrigerator is defined as the ratio of heat removed from the cold reservoir to the work input. Rearranging the formula gives the work required as heat removed divided by K.

Relevant Information

  • Refrigerator COP (K) = Qc / W
  • Heat pump COP = Qh / W
  • For refrigerator: Qh = Qc + W
2. A system absorbs 800 J of heat energy from the environment, and its internal energy increases by 500 J. What is the amount of work done by the system on the environment?
A
200 J
B
400 J
C
1500 J
D
300 J
Answer Explanation (Click to expand)
▼

Correct Answer

D) 300 J

Formula

First Law of Thermodynamics: ΔU = Q - W
Where ΔU = change in internal energy, Q = heat added to system, W = work done BY system

Process

  1. Given: Q = +800 J (absorbed by system), ΔU = +500 J
  2. Using first law: ΔU = Q - W
  3. 500 J = 800 J - W
  4. W = 800 J - 500 J = 300 J

Reasoning

According to the first law of thermodynamics, the change in internal energy equals the heat added to the system minus the work done by the system. Rearranging gives the work done by the system as heat added minus the increase in internal energy.

Relevant Information

  • First Law: ΔU = Q - W (W is work done BY system)
  • Sign convention: Q positive when added to system, W positive when done by system
  • If work is done ON system, W would be negative in this equation
3. A Carnot engine absorbs 200 cal of heat from a heat source at 400 K and rejects 150 cal of heat to a heat sink. What is the temperature of the heat sink?
A
400 K
B
200 K
C
150 K
D
300 K
Answer Explanation (Click to expand)
▼

Correct Answer

D) 300 K

Formula

For Carnot engine: Qh/Qc = Th/Tc
Where Qh = heat absorbed from hot reservoir, Qc = heat rejected to cold reservoir

Process

  1. Given: Qh = 200 cal, Qc = 150 cal, Th = 400 K
  2. Using Carnot relation: Qh/Qc = Th/Tc
  3. 200/150 = 400/Tc
  4. 4/3 = 400/Tc
  5. Tc = 400 × (3/4) = 300 K

Reasoning

For a Carnot engine (reversible engine), the ratio of heat absorbed to heat rejected equals the ratio of absolute temperatures of the reservoirs. This relationship comes from the Carnot efficiency formula.

Relevant Information

  • Carnot efficiency: η = 1 - Tc/Th = 1 - Qc/Qh
  • Carnot engine is a theoretical maximum efficiency heat engine
  • All temperatures must be in Kelvin for thermodynamic calculations

4200 J

A
23.62%
B
42.86%

50%

70.25%

Correct Answer

B) 42.86%

Temperatures must be in Kelvin

Process

  1. Convert temperatures to Kelvin: Tc = 127°C + 273 = 400 K

7. A faulty Celsius thermometer reads 10° in melting ice and 90° in boiling water. At what temperature will it show the correct reading?
A
0°
B
25°
C
50°
D
80°
Answer Explanation (Click to expand)
▼

Correct Answer

C) 50°

Formula

For faulty thermometer: Tactual = a × Rfaulty + b
Where a and b are constants determined from known points

Process

  1. Faulty thermometer: At ice point (0°C actual), reads 10°
  2. At steam point (100°C actual), reads 90°
  3. Let actual temperature = T, faulty reading = R
  4. Linear relationship: T = aR + b
  5. Using points: 0 = 10a + b and 100 = 90a + b
  6. Subtracting: 100 = 80a → a = 1.25
  7. Then b = -10a = -12.5
  8. So T = 1.25R - 12.5
  9. For correct reading: T = R
  10. So T = 1.25T - 12.5 → 0.25T = 12.5 → T = 50°C

Reasoning

Find the linear relationship between actual temperature and faulty reading. Then find where actual temperature equals the faulty reading (T = R).

Relevant Information

  • Faulty thermometers typically have linear errors
  • The correct reading occurs where the faulty scale intersects the true scale
  • This is found by solving T = aT + b where T is the temperature
8. A heat engine performs positive work in each cycle; it is powered by heat, yet the engine absorbs no heat. Which law of thermodynamics does the engine violate?
A
Zeroth Law
B
First Law
C
Second Law
D
Third Law
Answer Explanation (Click to expand)
▼

Correct Answer

B) First Law

Formula

First Law of Thermodynamics: ΔU = Q - W
For a cyclic process: ΔU = 0, so Q = W

Process

  1. A heat engine that performs positive work (W > 0) in a cycle
  2. But absorbs no heat (Q = 0)
  3. From first law for cyclic process: 0 = Q - W = 0 - W = -W
  4. This would require W = 0, contradicting W > 0
  5. Therefore, it violates the first law of thermodynamics

Reasoning

The first law of thermodynamics (conservation of energy) states that energy cannot be created or destroyed. A heat engine performing work without absorbing heat would create energy from nothing, violating energy conservation.

Relevant Information

  • First Law: Energy conservation for thermodynamic systems
  • Second Law: Entropy of isolated systems increases; limits efficiency
  • Zeroth Law: Defines temperature and thermal equilibrium
  • Third Law: Entropy approaches zero as temperature approaches absolute zero
9. An ideal gas expands into a vacuum within a rigid container covered with a heat insulator. As a result, which of the following occurs?
A
No change in internal energy
B
Temperature decreases
C
No change in pressure
D
Phase change occurs
Answer Explanation (Click to expand)
▼

Correct Answer

A) No change in internal energy

Formula

First Law: ΔU = Q - W
For ideal gas: ΔU depends only on temperature change

Process

  1. Gas expands into vacuum: Free expansion
  2. No external pressure → No work done (W = 0)
  3. Container is heat insulated → No heat exchange (Q = 0)
  4. From first law: ΔU = Q - W = 0 - 0 = 0
  5. For ideal gas, internal energy depends only on temperature
  6. So temperature remains constant (isothermal free expansion)

Reasoning

In free expansion into vacuum, no work is done against external pressure. With thermal insulation, no heat is exchanged. Therefore, internal energy remains unchanged. For ideal gas, this means temperature remains constant.

Relevant Information

  • Free expansion: Expansion against zero external pressure
  • For ideal gas in free expansion: ΔU = 0, ΔT = 0
  • Pressure decreases in free expansion as volume increases
  • Real gases may experience temperature change in free expansion (Joule-Thomson effect)

Reasoning

During phase change at constant temperature, the change in entropy is the latent heat divided by the absolute temperature. For water at 100°C (373 K), the entropy change per kg is approximately the latent heat divided by 373 K.

Relevant Information

  • Entropy change for phase change: ΔS = Qrev/T
  • For water at 100°C: Lv = 2.26 × 10⁶ J/kg
  • ΔS per kg = (2.26 × 10⁶ J/kg) / 373 K ≈ 6060 J/(kg·K)

Work done: W = Q × V
Where Q = charge transferred, V = potential difference

Process

Reasoning

The work done in moving a charge through a potential difference is the product of the charge and the potential difference. This is the fundamental relationship between electrical work, charge, and voltage.

Process
