Atmospheric Composition & Constants

Air Composition: N₂ → 78.09%, O₂ → 20.95%, CO₂ → 0.033%

Total Mass of Atmospheric Gases: 5 × 10¹⁸ kg / 5 × 10¹⁵ tons

Atmospheric Pressure at Sea Level: 14.7 lb/in² or 1.033 kg/cm²

Standard Pressure (1 atm): 101.325 Nm⁻² = 760 mmHg = 760 torr = 1 bar

Standard Temperatures:

  • Room Temp: 25°C / 298K
  • Standard Temp: 0°C / 273K
  • Absolute Zero: -273.15°C / 0K

Important Correction: Aircraft Altitude

Commercial aircraft fly in the upper troposphere (8-12 km altitude), not the stratosphere. Only specialized aircraft reach the stratosphere.

Air Pollutants & Acid Rain

Natural Air Pollutants

CO (Carbon Monoxide)

From incomplete combustion, volcanic activity

CO₂ (Carbon Dioxide)

From respiration, decomposition, volcanoes

SO₂ (Sulfur Dioxide)

From volcanoes, organic decay

NO (Nitric Oxide)

From lightning, bacterial action

NH₃ (Ammonia)

From biological decomposition

Secondary Air Pollutants

Formed by chemical reactions in the atmosphere:

  • PANs (Peroxyacyl Nitrates): Contain -CO-O-O-NO₂ groups; major component of photochemical smog
  • O₃ (Ozone): Formed by NO₂ photolysis
  • H₂SO₄ (Sulfuric Acid): Formed from SO₂ oxidation
  • HNO₃ (Nitric Acid): Formed from NO₂ oxidation

Acid Rain Components

Acid rain contains these major acidic compounds:

  • H₂SO₄ (Sulfuric acid) - Primary component (~60-70%)
  • HNO₃ (Nitric acid) - Secondary component (~30-40%)
  • H₂SO₃ (Sulfurous acid) - Minor component from SO₂ dissolution
  • HCl (Hydrochloric acid) - From industrial emissions

Gases Present in PANs

Peroxyacyl Nitrates contain various organic compounds including:

  • Peroxyacetyl nitrate (PAN)
  • Peroxypropionyl nitrate (PPN)
  • Peroxybutyryl nitrate (PBN)
  • Methyl nitrate and other organic nitrates

Charles's Law: V ∝ T (Constant P)

Avogadro's Law: V ∝ n

Ideal Gas Equation: PV = nRT

Kinetic Energy of Gas: PV = 1/3 mNC²

Dalton's Law of Partial Pressure: P_total = P₁ + P₂ + ...

Biological Impact of Heavy Metals:

Lead (Pb) Effects: Bluish-black gums, inhibits hemoglobin production, causes anemia, impairs brain development in children

Cadmium (Cd) Effects: Impairs lung function, causes osteoporosis and Itai-Itai disease

Arsenic (As) Effects: Interferes with enzymes, causes abdominal pain, skin cancer, and Blackfoot disease

Lewis Acids & Bases

Lewis Acids: Electron pair acceptors

CO₂ (Carbon Dioxide)

Can accept electron pairs into empty p-orbitals

SO₂ (Sulfur Dioxide)

Empty d-orbitals can accept electron pairs

BF₃ (Boron Trifluoride)

Classic Lewis acid with empty p-orbital

AlCl₃ (Aluminum Chloride)

Used in Friedel-Crafts reactions

Cu²⁺ (Copper Ion)

Transition metal ions act as Lewis acids

Lewis Bases

Electron pair donors:

  • NH₃ (Ammonia) - Lone pair on nitrogen
  • H₂O (Water) - Lone pairs on oxygen
  • OH⁻ (Hydroxide ion) - Extra electron pair
  • CN⁻ (Cyanide ion) - Lone pair on carbon

Permanent Basicity:

Compounds that retain their basicity in aqueous solution:

  • NaOH, KOH (Strong bases)
  • Ca(OH)₂ (Moderately strong base)
  • These completely dissociate in water

Temporary Basicity:

Compounds that lose basicity under certain conditions:

  • CaCO₃, MgCO₃ (Carbonates)
  • NaHCO₃ (Bicarbonate)
  • These decompose or react to lose basic properties

Classification of Organic Compounds

Organic compounds are primarily classified into two types:

Acyclic/Aliphatic: Open-chain compounds

Cyclic: Closed-chain compounds, further divided into:

  • Homocyclic/Carbocyclic: Ring made only of Carbon (e.g., Alicyclic and Aromatic)
  • Heterocyclic: Ring containing atoms other than Carbon (e.g., Pyridine, Furan)

Types of Isomerism:

  • Structural: Chain, Position, Functional Group, Metamerism, and Tautomerism
  • Stereo (3D): Geometric (Cis-Trans) and Optical

Battery & Fuel Cell Details

Lead-Acid Storage Battery

  • Cells: 6 cells in series in a car battery
  • Voltage: 2V per cell (Total 12V)
  • Internal Resistance: 0.001 Ω
  • Anode: Pb (Lead) powder
  • Cathode: PbO₂ powder
  • Electrolyte: H₂SO₄ (Density → 1.29 g/cm³)
  • Recharging: Recharged when EMF < 1.17V

Lithium & Lithium-Ion Batteries

  • Primary Lithium: Non-rechargeable. Anode: Li, Cathode: MnO₂
  • Secondary Lithium-Ion: Rechargeable
  • Anode: Graphite sheet with Lithium atoms (LiₓC₆)
  • Cathode: LiMn₂O₄, LiCoO₂, or LiFePO₄
  • Electrolyte: Organic solvent (Dimethyl carbonate + Ethyl methyl carbonate) + LiPF₆

Electrochemistry Formulas

Resistance

R = ρl/A
ρ = resistivity, l = length, A = area

Specific Conductance

K = 1/ρ = l/RA
Reciprocal of resistivity

Equivalent Conductance

λ = K × 1000/C
C = gram equivalent/liter

Faraday's First Law

W = ZIt = ZQ
W = mass deposited, Z = electrochemical equivalent

Electrochemical Equivalent

Z = Atomic Mass/(Valency × 96500)

Cell EMF

E_cell = E_anode(ox) + E_cathode(red)

Important Equations

  • Gibbs Free Energy: ΔG = ΔH - TΔS
  • Nernst Equation: E_cell = E⁰_cell - (0.0592V/n) × log Q
  • Relationship: ΔG⁰ = -nFE⁰
  • Faraday Constant: F = 96500 C/mol

Sample Solved Problems

Problem 01: Silver Deposition

A current of 0.25 A is passed through AgNO₃ for 10 mins, depositing 0.1679g of Ag. If 0.20 A is passed for 50 mins, how much Ag will deposit?

Using the ratio: W₂/W₁ = I₂t₂/I₁t₁

W₂ = 0.1679 × (0.20 × 50 × 60) / (0.25 × 10 × 60)

W₂ = 0.1679 × (600) / (150)

W₂ = 0.1679 × 4

W₂ = 0.6716g of Ag

Problem 02: Electrochemical Equivalent

4g of Ag is deposited by 2A current in 30 mins. What is the electrochemical equivalent (Z) of Ag?

Using Faraday's First Law: Z = W/(It)

Time in seconds: 30 mins = 30 × 60 = 1800s

Z = 4 / (2 × 1800)

Z = 4 / 3600

Z = 1.11 × 10⁻³ g/C

Problem 03: Time Calculation for Deposition

How long must 5.0 A current be passed through Cr₂(SO₄)₃ to deposit 2.5g of Cr? (Atomic mass Cr = 52)

Using formula: t = (W × n × F) / (M × I)

Valency of Cr in Cr₂(SO₄)₃: Cr³⁺, so n = 3

Faraday constant F = 96500 C/mol

t = (2.5 × 3 × 96500) / (52 × 5)

t = (723750) / (260)

t = 2783.65 seconds

t ≈ 46.39 minutes

Electrochemistry MCQs with Answers

1. Which of the following is NOT a strong electrolyte?
A) NaCl
B) HCl
C) KOH
D) CH₃COOH
Answer: D) CH₃COOH is a weak electrolyte that partially dissociates in solution.
2. In a lead-acid battery, what is the electrolyte?
A) KOH solution
B) H₂SO₄ solution (density 1.29 g/cm³)
C) LiPF₆ in organic solvent
D) NaCl solution
Answer: B) Lead-acid batteries use H₂SO₄ solution as electrolyte with density around 1.29 g/cm³.
3. What is the voltage of a single cell in a lead-acid battery?
A) 1.5V
B) 2.0V
C) 3.6V
D) 1.2V
Answer: B) Each cell in a lead-acid battery produces 2.0V. Six cells in series give 12V.
4. Which material is commonly used in salt bridges?
A) Sugar solution
B) KCl or KNO₃
C) Pure water
D) Ethanol
Answer: B) Salt bridges use electrolytes like KCl, KNO₃, NH₄NO₃, or Na₂SO₄ to maintain electrical neutrality.
5. What is the primary reference electrode in electrochemistry?
A) Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE)
B) Calomel electrode
C) Silver-Silver Chloride electrode
D) Glass electrode
Answer: A) Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE) is the primary reference with E⁰ = 0.00 V.
6. According to Faraday's First Law, the mass deposited (W) is proportional to:
A) Voltage applied
B) Current × Time (I × t)
C) Resistance of the solution
D) Temperature
Answer: B) Faraday's First Law: W = ZIt, so mass deposited is proportional to current × time.
7. Which of these is a non-electrolyte?
A) HCl
B) NaOH
C) CH₃COOH
D) Sucrose (C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁)
Answer: D) Sucrose is a non-electrolyte. It does not dissociate into ions in solution.
8. In the Nernst equation, what does 'n' represent?
A) Concentration of ions
B) Number of electrons transferred
C) Number of moles
D) Temperature in Kelvin
Answer: B) In the Nernst equation, n represents the number of electrons transferred in the redox reaction.
